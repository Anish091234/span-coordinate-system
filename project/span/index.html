SPAN — Solar Positional Address Notation

Every craft carries one hierarchical address. The dotted path names the chain of bodies it is nested within — like a network address — and the leaf coordinates are expressed in whichever frame is currently active: the deepest body whose sphere of influence (SOI) contains the craft. Climb out and the address shortens; fall into a body's SOI and it grows.

Why three coordinate types? Each scale has a job. Angles + AU describe where you are around the Sun; spherical range/bearing matches how spacecraft hold and change orbits; Cartesian metres give the precision needed metres from a surface. SPAN lets a craft speak whichever dialect its situation demands — while the path keeps every frame unambiguously linked back to the Sun.

Leaving a surface? Within one body-radius of a planet or moon's surface, the active frame switches to a geodetic regime — latitude · longitude · altitude — and the view zooms to the surface with a lat/long graticule and a live ground-track point. Climb back out and it reverts to the orbital (range/azimuth/elevation) regime automatically.

Distances, radii and sphere-of-influence radii are approximate and illustrative — sized for clarity, not navigation. SOI ≈ a·(m/M)2/5. Solar-system view uses a compressed (√) radial scale so all eight planets are visible at once.
