SPAN — Solar Positional Address Notation

Every craft carries one hierarchical address. The dotted path names the chain of bodies it is nested within — like a network address — and the leaf coordinates are expressed in whichever frame is currently active: the deepest body whose sphere of influence (SOI) contains the craft. Climb out and the address shortens; fall into a body's SOI and it grows.

SOL.TER.LUN  ⟨ X +812.4  Y −1203.0  Z +44.6 km ⟩
└─ path: nested Sun → Terra → Luna        └─ coordinates in the active (Luna) frame

A right-handed metric grid for the close-proximity precision of approach & landing.

Why three coordinate types? Each scale has a job. Angles + AU describe where you are around the Sun; spherical range/bearing matches how spacecraft hold and change orbits; Cartesian metres give the precision needed metres from a surface. SPAN lets a craft speak whichever dialect its situation demands — while the path keeps every frame unambiguously linked back to the Sun.

Leaving a surface? Within one body-radius of a planet or moon's surface, the active frame switches to a geodetic regime — latitude · longitude · altitude — and the view zooms to the surface with a lat/long graticule and a live ground-track point. Climb back out and it reverts to the orbital (range/azimuth/elevation) regime automatically.

ICRF Backbone & Frame Continuity

SPAN is a labeling system layered over a continuous J2000 ICRF (International Celestial Reference Frame) coordinate backbone — not a patchwork of disconnected frames. When a spacecraft crosses a sphere-of-influence boundary and the address updates from SOL.TER to SOL.TER.LUN, the underlying position vector expressed in ICRF Cartesian coordinates does not jump. The transition is a relabeling event — a convenience abstraction — not a physical discontinuity. Every SPAN address maps bijectively to a unique ICRF triple at a given epoch, making the system interoperable with any standards-compliant navigation software.

SpaceX Mission Story — Starship HLS Lunar Landing: When NASA's Artemis Starship departs Earth orbit, its SPAN address reads SOL.TER ⟨ r=700,000 km ⟩. The moment it enters Luna's 66,100 km SOI, the address grows a node: SOL.TER.LUN. From NRHO (70,000×3,000 km ellipse) to LLO (100 km circle) to touchdown, the address stays SOL.TER.LUN — only the leaf coordinates change. On the surface: SOL.TER.LUN ⌖ 89.2°S 0.0°E ↑0.0km. One address, unambiguous anywhere in the solar system. SpaceX Starlink, Dragon, and Starship all require exactly this kind of multi-frame position bookkeeping — SPAN makes the hierarchy explicit.
